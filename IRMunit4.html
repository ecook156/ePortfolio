Unit 1 - Network and Information Security Management: History & Definitions

Collaborative Learning Discussion 1

For this discussion we were required to read Glisson et al (2015) Compromising a Medical Mannequin and provide an initial post to discuss what major threats and vulnerabilities were discussed in the paper and how these could be mitigated against.

Initial Post

With the increased use and dependency on technology in medical settings, the potential for a malicious cyber-attack has become more of a concern (Glisson et al, 2015). The article on Compromising a Medical Mannequin identifies vulnerabilities of the iStan device and how these can be exploited.

There were two technical vulnerabilities identified in the paper, firstly, the network security solution consisted of wireless transmissions between the controlling laptop and mannequins internal access point. Using open-source tools, students were able to carry out a brute force attack to crack the PIN on the access point. Following a brute force attack of this nature there is a threat of tampering with the devices used by the training facility by making unauthorised changes to the information (Campbell, 2016) thus affecting the integrity of the iStan devices. The impact of these vulnerabilities being realised could influence medical professionals to incorrectly assess a situation (Glisson et al, 2015) Therefore mitigation steps could be put in place by disabling the WPS from the access point consequently removing the ability to use the Reaver tools used in the research.

Secondly, the network protocol used was found to be vulnerable to a denial-of-service attack. This attack made the iStan devices unable to receive any setting changes from the muse software causing a threat to the availability of the devices which would impact the medical staffs ability to train effectively. Householder et al (2001) argues that proper preparation is essential for effective detection and reaction of a denial-of-service attack therefore a two step approach should be taken to implement firewalls to prevent a Denial of service attack from occurring and also having a robust Disaster Recovery plan in place to quickly react and reduce the impact of the threat if an attack was successful.

Jonathan Ashmore

Hi Beran and Elizabeth, It was an interesting question regarding how to secure IoT devices. I think one of the best ways at least at a network level would be to isolate IOT devices from the main network into a subnet: network isolation or network segmentation. One way to do this is to use Virtual Local Area Networks (VLANs). VLANs allows all devices to be connected to a Local Area Network (LAN) yet limits the communication between devices as they are on subnets of the LAN (www.pearsonitcertification.com, 2015). In essence, each VLAN is it own broadcast domain allowing grouped devices to broadcast to each other regardless of the switch they are using (Study-ccna.com, 2016).

There are advantages to using VLANs: Scalable, Reduces security risks, Trusted, semi-trusted, and non-trusted devices can be isolated, Flexible- configured to users and not location, Relative ease of use in configuring VLANs

It is worth noting that the IOT device can still be beached; for example, the access point password could possibly be cracked, and the iStan-related wireless devices could be still DoS attacked. However, isolating the access point to a VLAN with only the medical device and relevant wireless equipment would greatly reduce breaches across the main LAN such as attacking other IOT devices, nodes, and networks.

Elizabeth Cook - response back to Beran and Jonathan

Hi both, thank you for your thought provoking posts regarding IOT devices. I agree that due to the size of the memory included in the devices it can be difficult to use stronger encryption methods. Ometov et al (2019) argues that the key to securing IOT devices is through authentication by various smart devices. Many IOT devices already use multi factor authentication therefore it is a method than can be included in the access to most devices. This will significantly reduce the possibility of a successful brute force attack as an attacker would need the secondary method of authentication which would be more difficult to intercept due to the short expiration of a one time pass. (Borgohain et al, 2015)

I agree with Jonathan that network segmentation is a good option to secure IOT devices by separating iStan devices onto separate VLANs it restricts the movement of a cyber attacker making it difficult to access multiple resources (Wagner et al, 2016). While network segmentation may not prevent a DoS attack it significantly reduces the impact to the business if one was to happen, when it comes to the use of medical devices, continuity of service and the ability to carry on with operations quickly after an attack is key.

Although brute-force attacks are fundamental and can be used on any encryption. As a result, most IoT device manufacturers do not feel responsible for the security of their products or their customers.
